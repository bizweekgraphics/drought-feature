Cattle Drain

Queensland, Australia

Photograph by Antoine Bruy

In Australia, the epic Millennium Drought that began in the mid-1990s was officially declared over in 2012. But in much of rural Queensland, there’s been no relief. About 80 percent of the northeastern state remains affected by water shortages, putting particular stress on the center of Australia’s cattle industry.

Ranchers are changing nearly all aspects of their business. To keep their stock manageable, they’re culling herds, planning to slaughter a record number of cows this year. That means they must try to find new markets to sell the meat, without driving down prices. Currently, Australia supplies more than a third of all imported beef to the U.S.

Ordinary grass has become a precious resource. Ranchers are killing kangaroos they say are devouring food their cows need. Without enough for their cattle to graze on, ranchers are tearing down mulga trees, whose leaves cows can digest. If even that falls short, some cattlemen truck their herds towards the coast or down south, where the drought is somewhat less acute.—Karen Weise

Fresno Is Drying

Fresno, California

Photographs by John Francis Peters

In 1871, Leland Stanford was building his railroad across California’s Central Valley when he spotted a lush wheat field amid the dry prairie. “Wonderful!” he said, according to lore. “Here we must build the town!” Fresno was founded the next year, and now houses half a million people. The area, which was so lush it was almost called Green Bush Springs, grew to become the center of the country’s food production, irrigated with canals fed by the dense Sierra snowpack. “In Fresno, water has always been easy to find and cheap to get,” says city spokesman Mark Standriff. Four years into California’s record drought, however, that’s no longer the case.

Farmers have left land fallow and drilled deeper into the ground to pump up water. Some of it is contaminated by nitrate plumes seeping in from agriculture. Don Wells, an aptly named conservation monitor with the city’s utility department, says businesses that depend on water for their core operations, like bottlers and golf courses, have wrung any excess out of their operations. Strip malls are tearing out decorative grass; restaurants are swapping out rinse nozzles; and “virtually every customer has a toilet issue,” Wells says. “We are in a dynamic change.” The city’s hospitals are now using recycled water.

In April, Governor Jerry Brown announced the first mandatory water restrictions in California’s history. Fresno’s goal was a 28 percent year-over-year reduction. By July, the city’s homes and businesses had cut water use by 31 percent. Here’s how they’re doing it.—Karen Wiese

Thirsty Megacity

Sao Paulo, Brazil

Photographs by Sebastián Liste

Almost a year ago, a severe water shortage drove a neighborhood in Sao Paulo to burn tires in protest as a drought stunned Brazil’s most populous city. The water crisis has not ended. In February of this year, the Cantareira, one of six reservoir systems that supplies the city of 21 million people, was down to just 6 percent of its 1.3 billion cubic meter capacity. Even after seasonal rains, the water level is still precariously low. The city’s utility, Sabesp, has about five months worth of water for its clients. “We burned through our reserves,” says Samuel Barreto, manager of water security at The Nature Conservancy of Brazil. “We’re still trying to get out of the eye of the hurricane.”

Sabesp has tried to ration water by reducing pipe pressure, a policy that has often left taps dry in hilltop homes and faraway slums. Sao Paulo’s governor has started work on twin 11-kilometer pipelines to siphon water from a richer and nearlyfull reservoir south of the city. That is not going to be enough of a solution if Sabesp does little with its existing infrastructure. Its old pipes are in disrepair: The utility loses 18.2 percent of its water each year to leakage.—Blake Schmidt
